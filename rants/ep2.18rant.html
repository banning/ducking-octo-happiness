____ To return to the irrelevant news posts, lets talk about String Theory and just what exactly a theory is. The difference between a scientific law and a theory is that laws explain how (Newton's law of gravitation describes what phenomenon will be observed when two bodies containing mass interact) whereas a theory explains why (Einsteinian relativity models gravity as curves in space-time, thus explaining why objects move towards one another). String Theory counts as a theory since it explains why we observe the phenomenon that we do in the universe (modeling matter and energy as one-dimensional vibrating strings). There is more, however. I was taught that a scientific theory has to be falsifiable. Attributing observed phenomenon to undetectable forces (such as spirits, God, pranic energy, telluric currents, etc) is not theorizing as such forces cannot be falsified by their nature of being undetectable. (I feel people who say that magick or other preternatural forces are real but beyond the detection of science are not analyzing the problem enough. If you are influencing the Universe in some way, it can be isolated and detected. That's a rant for another day, however...)

____ General relativity is a theory. Our species has observed light bending around objects in numerous places in the galaxy. If light was not manipulated as Einstein had calculated, his theory would have been falsified. String theory, however, has never made any falsifiable claims, and since the energies necessary to investiage the levels at which string theory deals are currently beyond the reach of our civilization, it's not going to be making any falsifiable statements in the near future. Granted I hold a Bachelor's of Science and not a Doctorate, but I feel for this reason the system of math labeled as "String Theory" is not rightfully a theory. Physicist Brian Greene, String Theory's ambassador to the masses, defends research into the field, saying that while it cannot be falsified, when one examines the mathematics, one intuitively feels that they must be right as they are so beautiful and work together so well (I don't have The Fabric of the Cosmos in front of me at the moment to get the exact quote, unfortunately).

____ So entire generations of theoretical physicists are pursuing a path because of aesthetics. Consider modern art. The typical response to Jackson Pollock or other abstract expressionism is to not consider it art at all. If one spends years studying the tradition, however, one can come to see just what those artists were trying to express with their blobs and thrown chunks of paint. Likewise, laypeople cannot understand the advanced mathematics involved in string theory, but after years of study, they can come to see the beauty hidden in the esoteric equations, and revel in the dance of numbers, just as one can find the beauty hidden in splatterings of paint. With this in mind, I declare that string theory is not a theory at all, but an unintentional art movement.
