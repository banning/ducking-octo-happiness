____ Grassroot groups and Senator Bob Casey are working to halt the construction of electrical transmission towers through Pennsylvania to locations in New Jersey and West Virginia that can't maintain their own power plants. Pennsylvania residents feel the towers are eyesores, however. There’s also the potential (but not verified) risk of increased childhood leukemia rates among people growing up near such constructions.

____ Pennsylvania is also on the front lines of wind power as the United States seriously begins considering moving towards a “green” economy. Two turbine farms (They’re not wind mills. The blades aren’t attached to a mill of any kind; they’re attached to a generator. They’re not milling any grain; they’re producing electricity. Learn the meaning of words before using them) already exist in Pennsylvania, and Not In My Back Yard groups are rallying to stop any more. Again, the eyesore claim is mentioned, although like the leukemia engendered by transmission towers, wind farms have their own real world problems: namely two of the largest bat kills ever found in the state from the blades striking the animals. Also like the leukemia though, whether or not the farms will have an impact on bat populations is still under study.

____ While there are other very good reasons to oppose such constructions and I'm not advocating for them, the eyesore claim is a bit subjective, and the mutable perception of reality is the topic on which these rants seem to focus. This person discusses how he enjoys the sight of transmission towers. Rather than being a blot on nature’s pristine landscape (Virgin wilderness? There’s nothing virgin about flowering trees and rutting deer), he sees them as the result of nature becoming self-conscious. Man is neither above the animals nor outside of the natural world. 2.4 billion years ago, anaerobic cells poisoned themselves with the oxygen they produced as a waste gas and caused an ecological crisis; fermentation without the aid of distillation can only produce beverages of about 24 proof with a maximum of 30 because the yeast kill themselves on their waste: ethanol. While our own release of toxins into the environment is certainly harmful, it shouldn’t be viewed as something unnatural. Those transmission towers and turbines are a part of nature, are the product of nature becoming intelligent. They are termite mounds on another level.

____ View them as the products of an alien intelligence. There’s a wonder in observing these structures towering over the forest and cutting through the hills. It’s like watching structures rising in Sim City or The Little People episode of the Twilight Zone (or its parodies on the Simpsons and Futurama). Such little, seemingly insignificant creatures can raise these works out of the minerals of the soil and use them to enhance their own existence. The fUSION Anomaly's author views the rows of transmission towers running through forests and deserts as physical manifestations of the noösphere, representations of our information age. The generation and delivery of electricity is one of the foundations of contemporary civilization. The United States is even embroiled in a guerilla war because of such reliance. Now the towers are seeming kind of ominous, but that’s due to wasteful practices and foolish behavior and not anything inherent in energy consumption.

____ Such structures needn’t be considered eyesores. They are simply another aspect of the affront to entropy that is life, providing the flow of resources that keeps a species thriving, not unlike the mycorrhizae networking the trees in the soil beneath.

____ Then again, there is all that leukemia and all those dead bats. What’s going to be thriving on our toxins once we kill ourselves off and no human ever again breathes the oxygenated air while sipping a can of beer?
