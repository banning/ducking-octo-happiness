____ Tomorrow is the fiftieth anniversary of the launch of Sputnik by the U.S.S.R. Since we have a base ten numerical system and five digits per appendage, that sounds significant. In the Natural History Museum in Washington D.C., one can see a nice display of humans wearing the skins of animals and holding sticks with sharp rocks tied to the ends by tendons. Down the street in the Air and Space Museum, one can see a mock-up of the Saturn V engines, which are attached to what is essentially a giant spear. Sputnik would never have been launched if Sergei Korolyov hadn't convinved the Kremlin to place a metal sphere on top of a missile being designed to drop a nuclear bomb on the United States. Along with the jet engine and the Internet, Sputnik and everything the space programs have contributed to human civilization (weather forecasting, global communications, medical monitoring devices, Velcro, etc.) are thanks to territorial instincts. Thankfully, cooler heads prevailed, and out of all the purposes our giant spears have been put to, their original intent has never been realized (at least in regards to nuclear weapons).
